How Modern Radars Saved the World


While in high school, I was drawn to the field of telecommunications, and how humans have managed to build such precise machines that can transport data through Electromagnetic waves from billions of kilometers away. The more I delved into this topic, the more complexities I found within it, and the more interesting it appeared. At some point, I stumbled upon the technological race between the U.S. and the Soviet Union during the Cold War, and how that race sparked a period of innovation from both ends, which gave rise to incredible never-before-seen technologies in most areas of technology, from semiconductors, to large antennas that can look beyond the Earth’s horizon. This standoff between these two military superpowers essentially came down to who can build the radar with the most accuracy, resolution, range, and survivability. However, as with everything, economics is the deciding factor behind what project has the highest chance of succeeding. Given the might of the American economy, the Soviet Union realized that they were never going to beat the American MIlitary Complex with regards to all-around R&D, and attacking power. Thus, they shifted their doctrine to a “defensive” mode, where they focused most of their resources into developing a cutting edge, world-class air defense system, which could in-turn render all the time, effort, and money that American defense companies poured into better, faster, and more agile aircraft and bombers useless. In essence, these more powerful radars had the capability of not only looking far into the distance, but they had good enough resolution to detect what type of aircraft or projectile they were looking at. This gave rise to another niche area of military aircraft, called stealth technology, which helped the American military gain an information and strategic advantage over their adversaries. It’s easy to see how this quickly developed into a cat-and-mouse game, where each side continues to one-up the other by means of better technology. These new technologies made it much harder for both superpowers to strike first without the other side knowing until it was too late, which gave rise to mutually assured distraction (MAD) doctrine, which essentially assures full-scale nuclear war, given that once an ICBM is launched, the other side will know in advance (due to modern radar and satellite technology), and they will launch their own arsenal, thus giving rise to a full-scale war. Hence, modern radars (together with nuclear ICBMs) serve as deterrents to any sort of conflict, as it’s easy to see from past history – that conflict can quickly escalate in a blink of an eye
