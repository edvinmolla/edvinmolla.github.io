positive rate

this is an analogy i've come to adopt when it comes to taking things off the ground.

usually the first stage of getting good at executing on ideas is to gain momentum as quickly as possible in order to capitalize on the moental burst of ideas, energy, and enthusiasm.

while it's only natural for the dopamine rush to eventually dip, i've come to realize that it's better to take advantage from it while you have it.

i vividly remember resonating with this line while reading the Almanack of Naval Ravikant

inspiration is perishable - act on it immediately.

however, i've also come to learn that taking things off the ground is a totally different ballgame. similar to how an airplane first has to gain speed and momentum before starting to roll of the ground, it's the phase right after taking off and during climb, that most accidents happen, statistically. this must mean that climbing is a vital, and complex process, heavily prone to erros and failures, which makes the process of scaling your idea a tricky endeavour.

pilots have a special term to signal a normal, safe climb, which is said by the pilot monitoring the instruments. the term is positive rate, and signifies a normal, and expected climb rate.

now, pilots operate in a much more predicabtle environment than a new business does. a pilot usually has thousands of hours of expeirence with one specific type of aircraft. they're supposed to know it inside out, and they develop a feel for the aircraft that helps in them recognizing anomalies.

an idea, on the other hand, is like a baby. it comes into this world with aboslutely no protection agianst environmental factors. a new idea is easiest to kill in the earliest stages of its life, hence why one must act swiftly.

while one can get good at gaining momentum for new ideas, it becomes increasingly unpredictable to use a certain playbook as a standard approach toward scaling your idea. the main reason behind this is that there' an infinite amount of possibilities as to how your actions will turn out, and most ideas don't even make to to a far enough point where you're taking it off the ground, hence removing the opportunity for you to learn more about the process.

ultimately, it is hard. and it should be. every idea is inherently different. it is born out of a different pattern of thoughts, and it's acted upon with a different amount of experience and knowledge.

however, one must remember to look out for any anomalies indicating anything other than a positive rate, thus indicating a need for swift and delibarte action to try to save your idea from coming down crashing.
